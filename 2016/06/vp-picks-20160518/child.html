Running Mates From Swing States Don’t At All Guarantee Victory

In the below chart, a bolded entry means the running mate was from a swing state that year (see footnote for our definition of “swing state”). Not only do presidential candidates not choose swing-state politicians as their running mates all that often; swing-state choices also don’t necessarily lead to wins.

Notes

We developed a crude definition of “swing state” here: any state in which, in the two presidential elections preceding the election in question, that election itself, and the two following it (five elections in total — so for 1988, the years considered are 1980, 1984, 1988, 1992, and 1996), the margin between the two major-party candidates was less than 5 percentage points in two or more elections. In other words: a state in which it was at least somewhat common for elections to be close during that time (that is, in two out of five elections). For 2008 and 2012, the most recent 5 elections are used. It’s an imperfect definition (in 1988, for example, Texas becomes a swing state despite its former governor running for president), but it’s broad enough to catch borderline-swing states.
