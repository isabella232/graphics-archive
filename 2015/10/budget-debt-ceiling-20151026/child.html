Congress’ Big Fiscal Deadlines, Explained

Lawmakers have two huge looming deadlines coming up: The U.S. is about to (once again) hit the debt ceiling, and (once again) the government will soon have to pass a new appropriations bill or face a shutdown. Here’s what you need to know about both.

The Debt Ceiling

What Is It?

The limit on how much money the government may borrow. In March, Treasury hit its current $18.1 trillion cap and then began using “extraordinary” measures to cover expenses. Those options are running out. Treasury Secretary Jack Lew has said the U.S. will hit the debt ceiling on Nov. 3.
Congress must pass a budget by the Oct. 1 start of each fiscal year. On Sept. 30, Congress passed a continuing resolution, or CR, to continue spending through Dec. 11. When that day comes, Congress must authorize new spending, extend the CR or shut down “nonessential” government operations.

What Could Happen?

Unless Congress raises the limit, Treasury will have only incoming revenue from taxes and fees, but those wouldn’t be enough to pay all bills on time and in full. It’s not clear how Treasury would decide which bills to pay and which to delay.
If Congress cannot agree on a budget, then a government shutdown begins. This has happened on a number of occasions in recent decades, with the most recent shutdown occurring two years ago and lasting 16 days.

What Are The Disagreements?

The White House and Democrats want to raise the ceiling to preserve the United States’ credit rating. Always paying bills on time allows Treasury to borrow at very low interest rates. Republicans don't like the idea of defaulting, but they see the debt ceiling debate as an opportunity to force the White House to accept deeper spending cuts and other reforms.
The Obama administration is seeking cuts in farm subsidies and higher taxes for investment fund managers. Republicans are asking for changes in entitlement payments, and they have raised other issues, such as funding for Planned Parenthood. The two sides have also differed over whether and how to lift sequestration caps on defense and discretionary spending. It has been a stalemate so far.

How Are These Different?

Raising the debt ceiling allows the U.S. to pay its existing bills. Raising it doesn't authorize new spending; rather, it allows the government to keep borrowing to pay for things it already passed. A budget authorizes spending. Without passing a budget, the government would have to stop performing some functions.

How Are These Related?

Raising the debt ceiling does not increase the debt or create new spending. However, when a budget causes the government to spend more than the government takes in, it can mean more borrowing, which can inch the U.S. closer to a future debt ceiling.

Who Could Be Hurt?

  • Social Security and Medicare recipients. The U.S. makes tens of millions of payments a month on programs like Social Security, veterans' benefits, and many others. Lew has warned that without raising the debt ceiling, the government couldn't keep paying those bills.
  • Bondholders. If the Treasury runs out of cash, it may not be able to pay public investors, including U.S. pension funds and retirees, as well as foreign governments.
  • Stockholders. Stock investors could lose money if a Treasury default rattles financial markets. Stocks took a big hit in August 2011, when a bruising debt ceiling battle led to a U.S. credit downgrade.
  • Homebuyers and other borrowers. If Treasury defaults even briefly, the result could be a lower credit rating. A lower rating typically means higher interest rates — and many consumer loan rates are pegged to Treasury rates.
  • Taxpayers. If Treasury ends up having to pay higher interest rates, then servicing the U.S. debt would become even more expensive. One way or another, taxpayers must make good on existing debt, and it’s easier to do that when Treasury can offer low interest rates.
  • Small businesses. During a shutdown, government agencies that help businesses, such as the Small Business Administration and the Federal Housing Administration, can’t process loans for construction and other purposes. During the 2013 shutdown, the Defense Department said its small-business spending dropped by 40 percent.
  • Tourism. National parks and the Smithsonian would shut down. During the last shutdown, about $500 million in visitor spending was lost.
  • Medical products and drugs. In a shutdown, the Food and Drug Administration can’t approve medical products, devices and drugs. The National Institutes of Health must stop much of its research.
  • Federal employees. They do not get paid during the shutdown and cannot do their work, such as processing tax refunds, preparing for flu season, inspecting food and so on.
  • Job seekers. The 2013 government shutdown killed the creation of an estimated 120,000 jobs, according to the White House Council of Economic Advisers.

Who's Involved?

Jack Lew

As Treasury secretary, Lew is one of the key people sounding the alarm on the debt ceiling. He has been warning Congress for weeks now that not raising the ceiling could be catastrophic. Should the U.S. hit the ceiling, it's not certain what would happen next. Treasury might then have to decide which bills to prioritize.

John Boehner

The outgoing speaker is reportedly close to reaching a deal with fellow congressional leaders on a debt ceiling hike and a two-year budget deal. Reaching a deal on either issue could create political fallout for Paul Ryan, who will almost certainly be the next speaker, but it would also relieve Ryan of dealing with two huge immediate problems.

Paul Ryan

If Boehner reaches a debt ceiling or budget deal, conservative members might force a Speaker Ryan to take a stand on whether he would have done the same, especially if Boehner is forced to pass it with Democrats' votes. If Boehner does not reach a deal, Ryan will have to wangle votes in a divided GOP caucus. Conservative members may punish him for cutting a deal with or relying too heavily on votes from Democrats.

Nancy Pelosi

If Boehner reaches out to Democrats to get the bill passed, he would reach out to Pelosi. To the extent that a bill needs Democratic votes to pass, that makes her an important figure.

Mitch McConnell

The leader of the GOP in the Senate, McConnell and other congressional leadership got off to a slow start on budget talks this fall. However, there have been reports that leadership is nearing a deal on both the budget and the debt ceiling.

Harry Reid

The Senate minority leader has been in talks with McConnell on a budget for weeks. He and other congressional leadership will be responsible for working out the broad strokes of a deal.

Freedom Caucus

Members of the conservative caucus have said they're opposed to a clean debt ceiling bill and want a budget to cut funding to Planned Parenthood. They could make life very hard for Ryan as speaker if he makes a deal they do not like, or if Boehner makes one that Ryan appears to support.

Credit Rating Agencies

Repeated threats to hit the debt ceiling (meaning the U.S. might not be able to pay all its bills on time) could cause agencies to lower their ratings on government debt, which could in turn lead to higher interest rates and make it harder yet to pay.

Appropriations Committee Members

Not a key figure in the debt ceiling negotiations
The House and Senate Appropriations committees will be tasked with hammering out the details of a budget once leadership reaches it.

President Obama

White House negotiators have been trying to hammer out a deal with congressional leaders. Because the president will sign or veto any eventual deals, he has a big say in setting the parameters.
