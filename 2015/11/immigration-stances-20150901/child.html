The 2016 presidential candidates have complicated, often-shifting views on immigration policy. Below, we tried to pin down exactly what they believe.

Notes

1. Clinton voted for border fence legisltion in 2006 but in 2008 clarified her stance, saying that "there may be places where a physical barrier is appropriate."
2. In this chart, candidates who support a path to citizenship automatically get a "yes" for supporting a path to legal status, since citizenship goes above and beyond legal status.
3. On all answers marked "Unclear," NPR reached out to campaigns to clarify their stances, unless otherwise footnoted.
4. Source: Email from campaign
5. In an email to NPR, the O'Malley campaign said, "E-Verify cannot happen in the absence of comprehensive immigration reform."
6. Sanders in 2013 voted for an immigration bill that (among many other things) mandated E-Verify, but his position is otherwise unclear.
7. Christie said in May that he thought a path to citizenship was extreme, but in July he stood by past comments supporting a path to citizenship.
8. Cruz has said he wants a "robust E-Verify system," but has been less clear on whether he wants it to be mandatory.
9. Fiorina's comments have indicated that she might be philosophically in favor of ending birthright citizenship, but she is also opposed to ending it because it would be politically difficult.
10. Fiorina has said E-Verify can't be mandatory because it doesn't work properly.
11. Huckabee has said he is in favor of a path for "DREAMers" and years ago said he was in favor of a pathway to citizenship for a broader population of unauthorized immigrants. He has seemed to distance himself lately, saying he opposes "amnesty."
12. Though he has been less clear on it recently and has also said he opposes "amnesty," Jindal in a 2013 op-ed said he supported a path to legal status and eventually citizenship "once the border is secure."
13. Kasich says he'd be open to a path to citizenship as a part of a broader immigration reform deal.
14. In an early 2013 speech, Paul seemed to back a path to citizenship for unauthorized immigrants, though he later insisted that was not what he was doing. A few months later, he moved to strip a pathway to citizenship out of an immigration reform bill.
15. Rubio has said he could support a path to citizenship but that it should be "10 or 12 years" (that is, after his hypothetical presidency) until that discussion happens, as New York Magazine writes.
16. In an Oct. 29 interview, Rubio said he wouldn't "immediately revoke" Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals and that he hoped it would only end as a result of immigration reform. But days later, he said he would revoke DACA. In addition, he has said he would revoke Deferred Action for Parents of Americans..
17. Trump wants to deport unauthorized immigrants, then allow some back in.
